1. Introduction

Why this matters: Most breaches involve human interaction. An informed employee is the strongest defense.

This module covers social engineering, phishing, vishing, impersonation, malicious links, and psychological tactics attackers use.

2. Social Engineering

Social engineering is manipulation ‚Äî attackers trick people into sharing data or taking actions that undermine security.

Common techniques:
  • Pretexting: Fake scenarios to gain trust (e.g., "I'm from IT").
  • Baiting: Offer something tempting (USBs, downloads).
  • Quid pro quo: Exchange (help for credentials).

3. Phishing

Phishing uses deceptive emails or websites to harvest credentials and deliver malware.

From: accounts@paypal-secure.com RED FLAG: domain spoof
Subject: URGENT: Confirm your account now RED FLAG: urgency

Dear Customer,
Please verify credentials by clicking the link below to avoid account closure within 24 hours.

http://bit.ly/secure-verify suspicious link

Red flags:
  • Generic greeting
  • Threats or urgent time pressure
  • Suspicious sender or domain
  • Unexpected attachments or shortened URLs

4. Voice Phishing (Vishing)

Vishing is phone-based social engineering. Attackers may spoof caller ID and pretend to be bank/IT support.

Scenarios:
  • Fake IT support asks you to install remote software ‚Äî never grant remote access unless verified.
  • Bank requests full account numbers or OTP ‚Äî call back on official number.

5. Impersonation Attacks

Attackers impersonate executives, vendors, or government officials to convince employees to transfer funds or share secrets.

How to respond:
  • Follow multi-step verification for wire transfers (call back known number + second approver).
  • Check email headers and domain carefully.
  • Escalate suspicious requests to security team.

6. Malicious Links & Downloads

Shortened URLs and domain spoofing hide malicious targets. Attachments may contain macros or executables.

Checklist:
  • Hover links to preview destination (desktop)
  • Prefer visiting official site yourself instead of clicking links
  • Never run unknown .exe or macro-enabled documents
  • Report suspicious attachments to IT

7. Scarcity, Urgency & Fear Tactics

Attackers intentionally create pressure to reduce critical thinking. Pause before you act.

Counter-tactics:
  • Take time to verify ‚Äî legitimate processes allow time
  • Ask "why the urgency?" and escalate
  • Involve colleagues or managers for second opinion

8. Best Practices & Reporting

  • Use strong unique passwords + a password manager
  • Enable MFA for all corporate accounts
  • Lock your workstation when away
  • Keep software and browsers updated
  • Report suspicious emails to security@company.local
